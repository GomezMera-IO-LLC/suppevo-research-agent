# Supplementation Strategy for Julie Gomez — Based on Genomic Findings

## Important Disclaimer

This is an informational document based on published research. It is NOT medical advice. All supplements should be discussed with Julie's healthcare provider (pediatrician, integrative medicine physician, or functional medicine practitioner) before implementation. Dosing, interactions, and individual responses vary. Some supplements may interact with medications or existing conditions.

---

## 1. Methylation Support (MTHFR C677T Homozygous)

Julie is homozygous for MTHFR C677T (rs1801133 GG/TT), which reduces her MTHFR enzyme activity by approximately 70%. This means she has impaired ability to convert folic acid into its active form (5-methyltetrahydrofolate / 5-MTHF). Methylation is critical for neurotransmitter synthesis, DNA repair, detoxification, and gene expression.

### Primary Supplements

#### A. Methylfolate (5-MTHF / L-Methylfolate)

**Why:** Bypasses the impaired MTHFR enzyme entirely by providing the already-active form of folate. Unlike folic acid (synthetic) which requires MTHFR conversion, 5-MTHF goes directly into the methylation cycle.

**Studied doses in ASD:**
- **Folinic acid (leucovorin): 2 mg/kg/day, maximum 50 mg/day** — This was the dose used in Frye et al. (2018), a randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial that showed significant improvement in verbal communication in children with ASD (Cohen's d = 0.70–0.91). [PMID: 27752075](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27752075/)
- James et al. (2009) used 400 mcg folinic acid twice daily combined with methylcobalamin, showing significant improvements in glutathione status. [PMID: 19056591](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19056591/)

**Forms to consider:**
- **L-Methylfolate (5-MTHF)** — The most bioactive form. Brands use names like Metafolin or Quatrefolic. Recommended as first-line for MTHFR TT genotype.
- **Folinic acid (leucovorin calcium)** — Prescription form used in clinical trials. Bypasses MTHFR but via a different pathway than methylfolate. Has stronger clinical trial evidence specifically in ASD.

**Practical starting approach:**
- Start low: 400–800 mcg/day of L-methylfolate
- Titrate up over weeks as tolerated
- Clinical trials used higher doses (up to 50 mg/day folinic acid) under physician supervision
- Monitor for overmethylation symptoms (irritability, anxiety, insomnia) — may indicate dose too high

**Important:** Avoid synthetic folic acid in supplements and fortified foods when possible. Julie's MTHFR homozygous status means unmetabolized folic acid can accumulate and potentially block folate receptors.

#### B. Methylcobalamin (Methyl-B12)

**Why:** Methylcobalamin is a direct methyl donor that works synergistically with methylfolate in the methylation cycle. It's needed for the enzyme methionine synthase to convert homocysteine back to methionine.

**Evidence in ASD:**
- Hendren et al. (2016) conducted a randomized placebo-controlled trial of methyl-B12 (75 mcg/kg subcutaneous every 3 days) in children with ASD, showing improvements in clinician-rated ASD symptoms. [PMID: 26889605](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26889605/)
- Corejova et al. (2022) reported improvements in clinical and psychological profiles after methylcobalamin syrup administration. [PMID: 35631176](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35631176/)
- James et al. (2009) combined methylcobalamin (75 mcg/kg, 2x/week) + folinic acid (400 mcg, 2x/day) for 3 months with significant improvements in glutathione redox status. [PMID: 19056591](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19056591/)

**Forms and dosing:**
- **Sublingual methylcobalamin:** 1000–5000 mcg/day (sublingual absorption bypasses GI issues)
- **Methyl-B12 injections:** 64.5–75 mcg/kg every 3 days (used in clinical trials; requires prescription)
- **Methylcobalamin syrup:** Available as OTC supplements, easier for children

**Note:** Avoid cyanocobalamin — it requires extra methylation steps to become active, counterproductive for someone with impaired methylation.

#### C. Riboflavin (Vitamin B2)

**Why:** Riboflavin is a cofactor for MTHFR. Even with reduced activity from the C677T variant, B2 helps maximize whatever residual enzyme activity exists.

**Dosing:** 25–50 mg/day (well tolerated; excess is excreted in urine)

#### D. Pyridoxal-5-Phosphate (P5P / Active B6)

**Why:** P5P is the active form of B6, needed for over 100 enzymatic reactions including neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, GABA). It works within the methylation cycle and supports transsulfuration (glutathione production).

**Dosing:** 25–50 mg/day of P5P (the active form avoids needing liver conversion)

**Caution:** High-dose B6 (>200 mg/day of pyridoxine) can cause peripheral neuropathy. P5P at moderate doses is generally safer.

## 2. GABA Support (rs1014531 AA — ABAT Region)

Julie's unique homozygous variant near the ABAT gene (which codes for the enzyme that degrades GABA) may affect her inhibitory neurotransmitter balance. The excitatory/inhibitory imbalance hypothesis is central to ASD neurobiology.

### Primary Supplements

#### A. Magnesium (Glycinate or Threonate)

**Why:** Magnesium is a natural NMDA receptor antagonist — it blocks excitatory glutamate signaling and promotes inhibitory (calming) pathways. It also supports GABA receptor function and is a cofactor for hundreds of enzymatic reactions including many in the methylation cycle.

**Evidence:**
- Butler (2019) proposed magnesium supplementation as a potential treatment for neurodevelopmental features, particularly in the context of chromosome 15q11.2 microdeletion and ASD. [PMID: 31207912](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31207912/)
- Magnesium deficiency is common in ASD and associated with behavioral symptoms

**Forms (ranked by relevance):**
| Form | Why | Dose |
|------|-----|------|
| **Magnesium L-threonate** | Crosses blood-brain barrier; shown to increase brain magnesium levels | 1000–2000 mg Magtein (provides ~144 mg elemental Mg) |
| **Magnesium glycinate** | Well absorbed, calming (glycine is itself an inhibitory neurotransmitter), gentle on stomach | 200–400 mg elemental Mg/day |
| **Magnesium taurate** | Combines Mg with taurine (another GABAergic amino acid) | 200–400 mg elemental Mg/day |

**Practical approach:**
- Start with magnesium glycinate at bedtime (promotes sleep)
- Consider adding magnesium threonate in the morning for cognitive support
- Total elemental magnesium target: 5–10 mg/kg/day for children (consult provider)

#### B. L-Theanine

**Why:** An amino acid from green tea that increases GABA, serotonin, and dopamine in the brain. Promotes calm alertness without sedation. Crosses the blood-brain barrier.

**Dosing:**
- Children: 100–200 mg, 1–2x daily
- Can be taken as needed for anxiety/overstimulation
- Very safe profile; no known serious side effects

#### C. Taurine

**Why:** Taurine is an inhibitory amino acid that modulates GABA-A receptor activity, supports neuronal membrane stability, and acts as an antioxidant. It also supports bile acid synthesis and detoxification.

**Dosing:**
- Children: 250–500 mg/day
- Can be combined with magnesium (many forms like Mg taurate deliver both)

#### D. Pharma-GABA (Natural GABA)

**Why:** Produced by fermentation (Lactobacillus hilgardii), Pharma-GABA can cross the blood-brain barrier more effectively than synthetic GABA. Promotes relaxation and reduces stress markers.

**Dosing:**
- 100–200 mg as needed (typically 1–3x daily)
- Works quickly (within 30–60 minutes)
- Best for acute anxiety/stress rather than long-term baseline

**Note:** The ability of oral GABA to cross the blood-brain barrier is debated in the literature. Some researchers suggest it works peripherally via the enteric nervous system. Magnesium and L-theanine have stronger evidence for modulating central GABA activity.

## 3. Dopamine Optimization (COMT Val/Val + rs6269 AA)

Julie's genotype creates a high-activity COMT enzyme that degrades dopamine rapidly, particularly in the prefrontal cortex. This results in lower baseline dopamine availability, which can affect executive function, working memory, motivation, and attention.

### Strategy: Support Dopamine Synthesis + Slow Degradation

#### A. Precursor Support

##### N-Acetyl-L-Tyrosine (NALT) or L-Tyrosine

**Why:** Tyrosine is the direct amino acid precursor to dopamine (Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine). With high COMT activity depleting dopamine faster, ensuring adequate precursor availability helps maintain production.

**Dosing:**
- **L-Tyrosine:** 250–500 mg/day for children (on empty stomach, morning)
- **N-Acetyl-L-Tyrosine (NALT):** More bioavailable form; 150–350 mg/day
- Best taken in the morning (away from protein meals for absorption)
- Avoid evening dosing (can be stimulating)

**Caution:** If Julie shows any signs of anxiety or overstimulation, reduce dose or discontinue. Tyrosine can increase norepinephrine too.

##### Iron

**Why:** Iron is a critical cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase — the rate-limiting enzyme that converts tyrosine to L-DOPA (the precursor to dopamine). Iron deficiency, common in children, directly impairs dopamine synthesis.

**Approach:**
- Test ferritin levels first (optimal for neurodevelopment: >40–50 ng/mL)
- Supplement only if levels are suboptimal
- Preferred form: Iron bisglycinate (gentle, well-absorbed)
- Dose: Per physician based on labs

#### B. Cofactors for Dopamine Synthesis

##### Vitamin D3

**Why:** Vitamin D regulates the expression of tyrosine hydroxylase (the rate-limiting enzyme for dopamine synthesis) in the brain. It also modulates immune function — relevant given Julie's HLA variant (rs660895 AA).

**Dosing:**
- Test 25-OH vitamin D levels first
- Target: 40–60 ng/mL
- Typical supplementation: 1000–2000 IU/day for children (adjust based on labs)
- Take with fat-containing food for absorption
- Julie's VDR gene variants (rs731236 AG) suggest moderate receptor activity

##### Zinc

**Why:** Zinc is required for dopamine transporter function and modulates NMDA receptors. Low zinc is associated with attention and behavioral issues in children.

**Dosing:**
- 10–15 mg/day elemental zinc (age-dependent)
- Best forms: Zinc picolinate, zinc glycinate
- Take away from iron supplements (they compete for absorption)
- Balance with copper (15:1 zinc-to-copper ratio)

#### C. Slow Dopamine Degradation (COMT Inhibition)

##### Magnesium

**Why:** Magnesium is a natural COMT inhibitor. It competes with the magnesium-dependent COMT enzyme for binding, slightly slowing dopamine breakdown. This directly addresses Julie's high-COMT-activity genotype.

**Approach:** Already recommended in the GABA section — serves double duty here.

##### Green Tea Extract / EGCG (optional, for older adolescents/adults)

**Why:** EGCG (epigallocatechin gallate) is a mild natural COMT inhibitor. It can modestly slow the degradation of dopamine and other catechols.

**Dosing (adolescent/adult):** 100–200 mg EGCG
**Note:** May not be appropriate for younger children. Discuss with provider regarding age-appropriateness.

#### D. Phosphatidylserine (PS)

**Why:** PS is a phospholipid critical for neuronal membrane structure and cell signaling. It supports dopamine release and receptor sensitivity, and has been studied in ADHD (which shares attention-related features with ASD).

**Dosing:** 100–200 mg/day

## 4. Additional Synergistic Support

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (DHA + EPA)

**Why:** DHA is the predominant structural fatty acid in brain membranes and supports synaptic function (relevant given Julie's SNAP25 variant). EPA has anti-inflammatory properties. Julie's FADS genotype (rs174546 CT) suggests moderate capacity to synthesize DHA from plant-based omega-3s, making direct supplementation beneficial.

**Evidence:** Multiple RCTs have studied omega-3s in ASD with mixed but promising results, particularly for irritability and hyperactivity. [PMID: 30744880](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30744880/)

**Dosing:**
- DHA: 500–1000 mg/day
- EPA: 250–500 mg/day
- Look for triglyceride-form fish oil (better absorption)
- Algal DHA is an alternative (vegetarian source)

### N-Acetylcysteine (NAC)

**Why:** NAC is the precursor to glutathione — the body's master antioxidant. Given Julie's MTHFR status (impaired methylation reduces glutathione production via the transsulfuration pathway), NAC provides an alternative route to boost glutathione. It also modulates glutamate signaling and may reduce irritability.

**Evidence:**
- Pesko et al. (2020) reported decreased irritability in youth with ASD using NAC supplementation. [PMID: 32071590](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32071590/)
- Schiavi et al. (2022) showed NAC mitigated social dysfunction in an autism model by normalizing glutathione imbalance. [PMID: 35280167](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35280167/)

**Dosing:**
- 600–900 mg/day (start at 600 mg, can increase)
- Take away from meals for best absorption
- Can cause GI upset initially — start low and titrate

### Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin)

**Already mentioned above** — serves as MTHFR cofactor AND supports mitochondrial energy production (relevant for neurodevelopment).

## 5. Implementation Strategy

### Phase 1 — Foundation (Weeks 1–4)
Start with the most critical and well-tolerated supplements:

| Supplement | Dose | Timing | Rationale |
|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| L-Methylfolate | 400 mcg/day | Morning with food | MTHFR support (start low) |
| Methylcobalamin (sublingual) | 1000 mcg/day | Morning | Methylation synergy |
| Magnesium glycinate | 100–200 mg elemental | Bedtime | GABA + COMT + sleep |
| Vitamin D3 | 1000–2000 IU | With fatty meal | Dopamine + immune support |

### Phase 2 — Build (Weeks 5–8)
Add once Phase 1 is tolerated:

| Supplement | Dose | Timing | Rationale |
|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| Omega-3 (DHA/EPA) | 500/250 mg | With food | Synaptic + anti-inflammatory |
| P5P (active B6) | 25 mg | Morning | Neurotransmitter cofactor |
| Zinc picolinate | 10–15 mg | Away from iron | Dopamine + cognitive support |
| Riboflavin (B2) | 25 mg | Morning | MTHFR cofactor |

### Phase 3 — Optimize (Weeks 9–12)
Based on response, add targeted support:

| Supplement | Dose | Timing | Rationale |
|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| L-Theanine | 100–200 mg | Morning/as needed | GABA + calm focus |
| NAC | 600 mg | Away from meals | Glutathione + glutamate |
| L-Tyrosine | 250 mg | Morning, empty stomach | Dopamine precursor |
| Methylfolate dose increase | 800 mcg–1 mg | Morning | If tolerating well |

### Phase 4 — Advanced (After 12 weeks, with provider)
Consider if needed based on response:
- Folinic acid (leucovorin) at higher doses — requires prescription
- Methyl-B12 injections — if oral not achieving results
- Magnesium threonate — for additional brain-specific magnesium
- Phosphatidylserine — for attention support

## 6. Monitoring & Testing

### Recommended Labs (discuss with provider)

| Test | What It Reveals | Target |
|------|----------------|--------|
| Homocysteine | Methylation status | < 7 μmol/L |
| Methylmalonic acid (MMA) | Functional B12 status | Normal range |
| Ferritin | Iron/dopamine synthesis capacity | > 40–50 ng/mL |
| 25-OH Vitamin D | Vitamin D status | 40–60 ng/mL |
| RBC Magnesium | Cellular magnesium (more accurate than serum) | 5.0–6.5 mg/dL |
| Plasma zinc | Zinc status | 80–120 mcg/dL |
| Folate receptor antibodies | May guide folinic acid dose | Negative preferred |
| Organic acids (urine) | Metabolic markers, neurotransmitter metabolites | Provider interprets |
| Glutathione (reduced/oxidized ratio) | Oxidative stress status | High reduced:oxidized ratio |

### Signs of Improvement to Track
- Verbal communication quality and frequency
- Social engagement and eye contact
- Repetitive behavior intensity
- Anxiety levels
- Sleep quality
- Irritability/meltdown frequency
- Focus and attention span
- GI comfort (gut-brain axis)

### Signs to Reduce/Stop a Supplement
- Increased irritability or agitation (possible overmethylation from folate/B12)
- Insomnia or hyperactivity (possible excess dopamine precursors)
- Nausea or GI distress (try different form or lower dose)
- Skin reactions (rare; discontinue and consult provider)

## 7. Key Interactions & Safety Notes

| Combination | Note |
|-------------|------|
| Methylfolate + B12 | Always give together — methylfolate without B12 can trap methylation |
| Iron + Zinc | Take at different times (compete for absorption) |
| Tyrosine + MAOIs | Do NOT combine with MAOI medications |
| NAC + Nitroglycerine | Potential interaction — not typical for pediatric use |
| High-dose folate + anti-epileptics | Folate can reduce seizure threshold in some cases; discuss with neurologist |
| Magnesium + antibiotics | Space 2 hours apart |
| EGCG + iron | EGCG inhibits iron absorption — take separately |
